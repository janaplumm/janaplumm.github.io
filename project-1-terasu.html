terasu

Connecting career starters with industry mentors to build strong soft skills

In 2024, Australia's youth unemployment rate sits at 9.5% compared with the general unemployment rate of 4.1%. To tackle the United Nations (UN) Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 8, our group applied user research, systems thinking, and iteration to create a hybrid digital and physical solution.






Project Journey

Quick overview from start to finish

In your group, choose one of the 17 United Nations (UN) Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as your problem space. As part of the user experience/service design stream, you must include a combination of digital, spatial, and physical elements in your solution.

After conducting an initial brainstorm, we decided to choose SDG 8, which pertains to promoting sustained, inclusive, and sustainable economic growth, full productive employment and decent work for all. As final-year university students, we have a personal interest in this matter.

We also found that targets 8.5 and 8.6 are off track and require a breakthrough according to Monash University (2020).

Targets 8.5 and 8.6:
  • 8.5: By 2030, achieve full and productive employment and decent work for all women and men, including for young people and persons with disabilities, and equal pay for work of equal value.
  • 8.6: By 2020, substantially reduce the proportion of youth not in employment, education or training.

Therefore, we decided to focus on career starters to account for youth, school leavers, and people pivoting their careers.

Research was conducted using both secondary and primary methods. The desktop research started with a diverging process, exploring academic papers, statistics and news articles, and later converged by narrowing the focus for user research.

Overview of Desktop Research
  • Investigated the current state of the Australian labour market, finding an increase in youth unemployment from 2023 to 2024.
  • Discovered that 75% of Australian employers value soft skills as much as, or more than, technical skills.
  • Identified three significant issues through our desktop research: Skill development, access to information and technology, and recruitment challenges.
  • Tools such as the competitor analysis and stakeholder mapping helped us better understand the larger ecosystem.

Overview of User Research
  • Formulated four hypotheses focusing on the 'how' and 'why' based on our desktop research findings.
  • Conducted a survey (36 responses), 16 interviews, and an online ethnography targeting social media communities.
  • Employed research triangulation to gather data from anecdotal, cultural, and personal user insights.
  • Derived four key insights through analysing our findings using an affinity diagram.

After consulting with our peers and tutor, we chose to direct our attention to the key insight of "career starters need to feel confident in being able to express their authentic selves during the job hunt". This allowed us to begin designing for this specific user need. The design process involved three iterations, resulting in numerous brainstorming and ideation sessions over the course of 12 weeks.

Overview
  • After conducting research and reframing our original problem statement, we arrived at four initial concepts that were presented for feedback.
  • Using a design critique, we adapted the feedback and applied various methods, such as systems mapping, mood boarding, and sketching.
  • We created our first low-fidelity prototype for a concept titled CoMEtted.
  • We developed the prototype and following three rounds of user testing, we arrived at our final concept, called terasu.

Intermediate Stage: Design Methods and Testable Concept

We explored the new concept through mood boarding, brainstorming, mind mapping, and sketches. Our tutor introduced us to systems thinking. The systems map proved to be an invaluable tool, as it gave us a bird's-eye view of the interconnected relationships between the stakeholder groups within our problem space.

From there, we developed CoMEtted, a three-part physical and digital experience with the aim of inspiring users to self-reflect and motivate themselves to build their soft skills and confidence. The name is a play on 'me' and 'committed'.

CoMEtted involves three components, each offering a separate experience:

  • A smart mirror offering a reflection activity.
  • A motivational wall with encouraging quote projections providing a sense of community.
  • An app for long-term, personalised engagement, allowing users to set goals aligned with soft skills and assign a buddy for accountability.

The mirror and the wall would be situated on educational campuses.

End Stage: Design Principles and Final Concept

After three rounds of user testing, we arrived at our final design concept. This process involved several iterations and a name change. User testing revealed confusion around the name 'CoMEtted', especially from international students, and this led to the more abstract name of 'terasu'. Terasu means 'to shine' in Japanese and is inspired by the myth of the Japanese sun goddess, Amaterasu.

terasu offers a more simplified, purposeful experience compared to CoMEtted. Instead of three separate components, the wall of motivation and the mirror were combined into one installation. Rather than being a smart mirror, it now acts as a symbol for self-reflection. The app was adapted to involve mentorship from industry professionals rather than a buddy for accountability. This resulted in two apps, one for career starters and one for mentors. This concept is similar to Uber, which has separate apps for drivers and passengers.

User testing was not just vital for the final outcome, but for the entire process. It enabled us to gain a better understanding of our target audience and their needs. It also uncovered issues that we, as designers, were less likely to detect due to our own biases.

First Round

We tested a low-fidelity version of CoMEtted using a comprehensive testing protocol, which involved the think aloud method, interviewing, and a survey. Our analysis of the quantitative and qualitative data revealed important insights, including a need for clarity, personalisation, and enhanced interactions.

Second Round

We tested a mid-fidelity version of terasu in a lecture theatre using projectors to mimic the wall of motivation. The improved fidelity of the prototype allowed for participants to feel more immersed in the experience. While the second user testing round showed clear improvements from the first round, we uncovered new problems. These were more specific towards the transition from the physical experience to the digital experience. Also, the app onboarding process was too long and still included the buddy system.

The final user test involved a heuristic evaluation of the app, carried out by group member Morgan.
